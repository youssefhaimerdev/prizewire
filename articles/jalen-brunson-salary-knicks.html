From Second-Round Pick to $156 Million Man

In 2018, Jalen Brunson was selected 33rd overall in the NBA Draft by the Dallas Mavericks — the first pick of the second round, where most players disappear. Eight years later, he is the Eastern Conference Finals MVP, leading the New York Knicks to their first NBA Finals appearance since 1999, and earning $45.6 million this season on a four-year, $156.5 million contract extension he signed with New York in 2023.

The Brunson story is one of the great developmental arcs in recent NBA history. He was widely considered an overachieving reserve at Villanova. He was seen as a solid backup at Dallas. When the Knicks signed him as a free agent in 2022 for what many felt was an overpay, they were mocked by segments of the media. They have been proven comprehensively wrong. Brunson has become one of the best point guards in basketball and the unquestioned leader of a team that just swept the Cleveland Cavaliers out of the Eastern Conference Finals.

The ECF MVP Performance

Brunson's Eastern Conference Finals sweep of the Cleveland Cavaliers was a masterclass in clutch basketball. He averaged approximately 32 points, 7 assists, and 4 rebounds per game across the four games, consistently making the plays that mattered most in the fourth quarter. His ECF MVP award was essentially a formality — Brunson was the best player in the series by a significant margin.

The Knicks' supporting cast played their roles perfectly. OG Anunoby was a defensive force. Karl-Anthony Towns dominated the paint. Mikal Bridges provided secondary scoring. But every critical decision came through Brunson, who at 29 years old is playing the best basketball of his career at the right time.

What a Championship Means Financially

If the Knicks win the NBA Finals, Brunson will earn a $547,000 playoff bonus — the standard NBA champion player share — on top of his $45.6 million season salary. That bonus amounts to roughly 1.2% of his annual earnings. But the financial impact of winning goes far beyond the check: a championship-winning Brunson becomes one of the most marketable players in basketball's biggest market. His endorsement portfolio — currently including a variety of brands — would expand significantly. And New York, famously, rewards its champions.

Brunson vs. Wembanyama: The Finals Money Matchup

If the Spurs advance past the Thunder tonight, the NBA Finals will feature the most underpaid superstar in basketball (Wembanyama, $13.4M) against the highest-paid point guard of his generation in New York (Brunson, $45.6M). The contrast in contracts — one a second-round pick who became a franchise player, the other a generational talent on a rookie scale — will be one of the storylines of the series. Both will earn the same $547,000 if they win. Only one roster gets to hold the trophy.
