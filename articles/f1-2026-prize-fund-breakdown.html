How F1 Prize Money Is Distributed

Formula 1's prize money system is unlike any other sport. FOM (Formula One Management) distributes the fund to constructors only — not individual drivers. This means Max Verstappen, Lewis Hamilton, and every other driver receive zero from the FOM prize fund directly. Their earnings come from team salaries negotiated privately.

The fund has three main components: Column 1 bonuses (historical team loyalty payments worth ~$200M total, going to longest-established teams), Column 2 performance payments (based on Constructors' Championship standings), and Column 3 bonuses (additional amounts for top performers). The exact split changes annually based on FOM's commercial arrangements.

Why Even Last Place Gets $35 Million

The base participation structure guarantees every constructor a substantial payout just for fielding a competitive team. This floor — approximately $35 million — is why backmarker teams like Haas and Williams remain financially viable despite rarely scoring significant championship points. Without it, the grid would shrink dramatically as smaller teams couldn't sustain operations.

F1 vs. Champions League: The Biggest Prize Pools in Sport

F1's $1.1B prize fund sounds enormous — and it is. But the UEFA Champions League surpassed it in 2024-25 with a €2.47 billion ($2.71B) total pool across 36 clubs. In F1, money is concentrated among 10 teams (20 cars). In football, it's spread across dozens of clubs. Per team, the WCC winner's ~$147M is comparable to Champions League winner PSG's €144M — an interesting parallel between the two richest competitions in their respective sports.
