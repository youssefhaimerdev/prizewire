Kimi Antonelli 2026: 4 Wins, Championship Lead — On a $2M Salary

Andrea Kimi Antonelli is 19 years old, driving the fastest car on the grid, and has won four of the first five Grands Prix in 2026. He earns $2 million in base salary — less than some NFL backup quarterbacks. Here's the full financial picture behind F1's breakout story of the year.

The Most Underpaid Dominant Driver in F1 History

Through five races in the 2026 Formula 1 season, Kimi Antonelli has won in Australia, Japan, Miami, and Canada — four victories that have him in commanding control of the World Drivers' Championship standings before the Monaco Grand Prix on June 7. His car, the 2026 Mercedes W17, has benefited massively from F1's new technical regulations. But no regulation change explains what Antonelli is doing on track. This is a generational talent performing at a level that makes his paycheck look absurd.

His confirmed base salary for 2026 is $2 million — the same figure he earned as a rookie in 2025. That places him joint-19th on the 20-driver grid by base salary. For context: Antonelli earns less per race weekend than some F1 mechanics at top teams. His teammate George Russell earns an estimated $35 million. The driver he replaced, Lewis Hamilton, earns ~$50 million at Ferrari. The man currently leading the championship earns less than the team's hospitality budget.

Who Is Kimi Antonelli?

Born in Bologna, Italy on August 25, 2006, Andrea Kimi Antonelli was identified by Mercedes as a generational talent while still in karting. He won the Italian F4 and ADAC F4 championships in 2022 at age 15. Mercedes skipped him over Formula 3 entirely, putting him straight into Formula 2 in 2024 — an almost unprecedented vote of confidence. When Lewis Hamilton announced his departure to Ferrari, Antonelli was the only name Mercedes considered as his replacement.

He made his F1 debut in 2025 alongside George Russell. The 2025 season was a learning year — Mercedes' car was competitive but not dominant. In 2026, under entirely new technical regulations, the W17 emerged as the benchmark, and Antonelli immediately showed he could exploit it better than any driver on the grid. His tire management, racecraft, and one-lap pace have drawn comparisons to a young Michael Schumacher and a young Lewis Hamilton — though Antonelli himself dismisses comparisons, saying only: "I just try to go fast every lap."

The Monaco GP: History Waiting to Happen

Monaco is the most prestigious race in Formula 1. No driver has won five of the first six Grands Prix of a season since Michael Schumacher in 2004. If Antonelli wins Monaco on June 7, he will become the youngest driver ever to win the Monaco Grand Prix, and only the third driver to win five of the first six races of a season. The financial stakes are modest — individual race prize money in F1 goes to constructors, not drivers — but the sporting and commercial implications are enormous. A Monaco victory, at 19, would trigger renegotiation conversations that would make Antonelli one of the ten highest-paid drivers in F1 within a year.

The Salary Renegotiation Question

Antonelli's contract runs through 2027. His $2 million base salary was set when he was a promising rookie — before anyone knew he'd be leading the championship at 19. The contract includes performance bonuses that sources estimate could push his 2026 earnings to approximately $14 million if he wins the championship. But that's still a fraction of what the market would pay for a current WDC leader.

For comparison: Max Verstappen earns $70 million per year at Red Bull — the same Red Bull where Verstappen reportedly has an exit clause that triggers if he's outside P2 in the championship at the summer break. With Antonelli winning four races and Verstappen finishing third in Canada while struggling with the new regulations, the championship picture — and driver market — is shifting rapidly.
