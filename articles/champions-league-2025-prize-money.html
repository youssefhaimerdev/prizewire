Champions League 2024–25 Prize Money: PSG Wins €144.4M, Inter Milan €136.6M

Paris Saint-Germain won the 2024-25 UEFA Champions League, claiming €144.4 million from the competition's record €2.47 billion prize pool — the largest in football history. Here's how every euro was distributed.

How Champions League Prize Money Works

The 2024-25 UEFA Champions League introduced a new 36-team single-standings league phase, replacing the old group stage format. This structural change was the primary reason the total prize pool surged from €2.08 billion to €2.47 billion — an increase of roughly €400 million. Every participating club began with a base participation fee of €18.62 million just for entering the league phase. Money then accumulated based on results, coefficient rankings, and broadcast market pools.

Paris Saint-Germain topped the prize money table by winning the final at the Allianz Arena in Munich, beating Inter Milan. In addition to their participation fee and performance bonuses throughout the competition, PSG received the winner's trophy bonus, bringing their total to €144.4 million — the highest ever earned by a Champions League winner in a single season.

Why PSG's Total Reached €144.4 Million

The gap between PSG's total (€144.4M) and just the final winner's bonus (€21.1M) is explained by the accumulation of earnings throughout the competition: participation fee, per-match result bonuses across all league phase games, coefficient-based payouts reflecting PSG's historical UEFA ranking, and national broadcast market pool distributions. Arsenal, who reached the semi-finals, had already accumulated approximately £97 million despite not playing in the final — underscoring how early-round earnings dwarf the knockout bonuses in absolute terms.

Football vs. Other Sports: Where Champions League Ranks

At €2.47 billion ($2.71 billion), the Champions League prize pool surpasses Formula 1's $1.1 billion season prize fund to become the single largest prize pool in all of professional sports — though the comparison is complicated by how F1's money goes directly to constructors while UCL funds are distributed across 36 clubs. No individual club earns as much from one competition as the F1 champion constructor does. That said, for sheer collective prize money in one tournament, Champions League stands alone.
