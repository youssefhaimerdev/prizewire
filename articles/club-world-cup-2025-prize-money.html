FIFA Club World Cup 2025: Chelsea Win $125M from $1 Billion Prize Fund

Chelsea Football Club won the expanded 32-team FIFA Club World Cup held in the United States in June-July 2025, earning up to $125 million from a record $1 billion total prize fund — the largest in club football history.

Football's Most Controversial — and Lucrative — Tournament

The 2025 FIFA Club World Cup, expanded from 7 to 32 clubs and held across the United States in June and July 2025, generated significant controversy before a ball was kicked. Players, managers, and leagues complained about fixture congestion, the physical toll on players, and the lack of meaningful consultation. Crowd sizes were sparse for many early games. But the financial numbers were undeniable: a $1 billion total prize fund — more than the FIFA World Cup's $655M performance pool — made it the richest club competition in history.

Chelsea Football Club won the final to claim approximately $125 million — surpassing the €144.4 million that PSG earned from winning the Champions League in the same year when converted to USD at the prevailing rate. The victory prompted immediate debate: could the Club World Cup eventually rival or surpass the Champions League in financial and sporting prestige?

Club World Cup vs Champions League: Which Pays More?

Chelsea's ~$125M from the Club World Cup is comparable to PSG's €144.4M from the Champions League ($159M) — though the UCL total pool (€2.47B) is nearly 2.5x larger than the Club World Cup's $1B. The key difference: the Club World Cup has only 32 participants vs the UCL's 36, and the participation fee structure is far more generous per club in the CWC. For clubs from South America, Asia, or Africa — where Champions League entry isn't possible — the Club World Cup represents an extraordinary financial windfall.

The Legacy Question

Whether the Club World Cup maintains this prize structure long-term remains to be seen. FIFA's revenues have boomed, but the tournament faced genuine sporting criticism. Many of Europe's biggest clubs fielded weakened squads due to injury concerns, and attendance figures disappointed for non-marquee matches. The $1 billion prize fund was FIFA's way of ensuring buy-in from reluctant clubs and players. Whether it earns genuine sporting credibility — as opposed to financial compliance — will depend on whether future editions improve the competitive quality.
